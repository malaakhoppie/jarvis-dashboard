### LIQUIDITY TYPE 1 — The Unmanipulated Doji

`[VISUAL: Chart example of a clean unmanipulated doji sitting within a previous candle's wick]`

A doji that forms within a previous candle's wick. A pure, equal-force candle — no FU element, no manipulation. Just a clean balance.

Why is this major liquidity? Because retail traders learn that dojis signal reversals. They place stops just above or below it. Every technical analysis textbook teaches this. Banks know this. The concentration of retail stops sitting at this level makes it a premium target.

The critical distinction — and this is important because most students blur it — is between a TRUE unmanipulated doji and an Attempted FU. A true doji stays contained within the previous candle's wick. It has not taken any liquidity outside that range. If it has reached outside the previous wick even partially — it is an ATT FU, not a true doji. They are not the same. The implications for liquidity calculation are completely different.

The true doji is one of our highest-probability major liquidity targets. The ATT FU is minor. Confusing the two will cost you RR and win rate.

### THE FU CANDLE — The Most Important Formation

`[VISUAL: Classic FU candle — long wick in one direction, strong body close in the other]`

The FU candle does two things within the same candle:
- It takes liquidity in one direction — the wick reaches out, hunts the stops
- Then it closes strongly in the opposite direction — the body reverses

Both. In one candle. The wick is the hunt. The body is the true move beginning.

Two confirmations are required for a valid FU:
1. Takes liquidity — the wick reaches into an area of retail stop losses
2. Breaks structure — the close is in the opposite direction, breaking a previous high or low

The stronger the close — the less rejection wick on the body side — the stronger the signal. A strong full-body close tells you the banks committed. They are comfortable. They closed it hard because they wanted to.

What does an FU candle represent? Banks executing. They swept the liquidity. They filled their orders. They closed the candle showing true direction. The wick is their entry. The body is their statement.

**The FU wick becomes your Point of Interest.** Where did the banks enter? At the base of that wick. Where do banks re-enter? Often at the same level on a retest. A retest of the FU wick is one of your most reliable entry models.

There are specific types of FU candles you must distinguish:

**Strong FU (SFU)** — Full body close in opposite direction after taking liquidity. The best.

**Attempted FU (ATT FU)** — Started as an FU, took liquidity with the wick, but closed as a doji instead of strong reversal. The banks stepped in but did not commit fully. This creates minor liquidity — not a major target. Distinguishing ATT FU from a true unmanipulated doji is one of the most critical skills in this entire method. The ATT FU has a wick that took liquidity outside a previous range. The true doji does not.

**The 3-Candle FU** — The FU formation spread across three candles instead of one. The wick phase, the hesitation, the close — each on a separate candle. Advanced formation — the logic is identical, the execution requires recognising the spread.

`[VISUAL: FU candle down immediately followed by FU candle up — the negation]`

When banks form an FU in one direction — and the very next candle forms an FU in the opposite direction — this is a negation.

What happened? The banks attempted to move price in the first direction. Then they changed their mind — or the opposite side had more power. The second FU cancels the first. The power of the first move is exhausted. Change in order flow begins.

The negation is not just information — it is a trade. The wick of the negating candle becomes a Point of Interest. Mark it. When price returns to that wick area, you have a potential entry in the direction of the negation.

Negation rule for counting: if the first candle after an FU forms as an ATT FU (not a complete negation wick), the second candle can still count as the negation. Give it two candles to confirm before dismissing.

One-minute negations = potential entries. Four-hour negations = powerful directional signals. The logic is identical across all timeframes — only the weight of the statement differs.

### HCS — High Confluence Setup — The Most Powerful Formation

`[VISUAL: FU candle → retest candle → retest ITSELF forms a FU = HCS]`

When an FU candle is retested — and the retest itself forms an FU — you have an HCS.

The anatomy:
- First FU: banks moved. True direction established.
- Retest: retail attempts to reclaim. Price returns to the FU wick.
- Retest FU: the banks defend aggressively. They step in at the retest and drive price back in the original direction.

Three events. Three confirmations. All pointing the same way.

The HCS carries **double the strength** of a standard FU on the same timeframe. A 30-minute HCS is equivalent to a one-hour FU in timeframe strength. A one-hour HCS is equivalent to a two-hour FU. This is structural mathematics — two institutional confirmations stacked.

### THE MANIPULATION CYCLE — Phase 3 Entry

`[VISUAL: Diagram of 4 phases — Cluster → Sweep → Return → True Move]`

Every FU candle, in its mechanics, consists of four phases:

**Phase 2 — The Sweep.** Price moves through the cluster. Stop losses are triggered. Breakout traders enter in the wrong direction. Banks fill their orders using this stop-loss fuel.

This is how you enter as the FU is forming — not after it has already formed and moved.
